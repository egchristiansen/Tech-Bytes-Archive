# 1s A Pa$$wrd-less fUture* %realistic?

March 25, 2022

By Erik Christiansen

I've observed a trend over the past fifteen years. Every few years, there's a new proposal to eliminate our dependence on passwords and implement a new technology to secure our online accounts. Articles written in 2004, 2009, 2011, 2015, 2016, 2016, 2019, 2021, all outline the major flaws with passwords. (To be fair, the 2016 Tech Radar article argued it was unlikely that passwords would disappear in the short run). The pitch for eliminating passwords is always the same. Passwords are insecure and too burdensome to manage. Therefore, for the betterment of society (i.e. security), we need to implement a multi-factor authentication system that requires several other technologies to be broadly adopted.

I get it. People are lazy, they pick bad passwords, and they get hacked. Users who pick strong passwords can also get hacked through phishing scams where they willingly hand over their login information to scammers posing as "Windows support professions" or your bank. Two-factor authentication (using either SMS or an authenticator app) is still vulnerable to phishing scams.

In a recent Wired article, Lily Hay Newman discusses the release of a new white paper by The FIDO Alliance, an industry association focused on improving web-based security. Wired points out that the white paper is a conceptual piece, not a technical one. She says the following.

FIDO white paper

It's good practice to go to the source. What exactly does this white paper say about passwords? FIDO introduces itself in the following way.

Translation: "FIDO cares about your security and we're finally on the cusp of implementing a replacement for passwords."

FIDO provides a short history on digital security, drawing attention to "Personal Identity Verification" (PIV) which was a precursor to passwords. PIV was a more secure solution but required specialized hardware - a smart card and card reading device. Their thesis is that passwords have never been top of the heap in terms of security, but they are more convenient for the average person. Interestingly, they also discuss hardware-based token systems - in which a portable device would "periodically-changing code to the user" - to today's two-factor authentication apps on smartphones. FIDO posits that while two-factor security is better than just a password, it's still susceptible to phishing attacks. For example, if a scammer - posing as an employee of Microsoft Security or your bank - got someone on the phone, they could convince the user to hand over both their password and two-factor token to log into your account. FIDO describes security as a continuum from least secure to most secure, using the guidance outlined by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST). NIST ranks security methods by "authenticator assurance level" or 'AAL.' According to NIST, passwords live in AAL1 category (i.e. least secure). AAL2 requires some sort of multi-authentication system; using a password and two-factor authentication via an app would qualify. AAL3 is similar to AAL2 except that multi-authentication systems must be hardware-based and phishing resistant. The white paper goes on to describe how FIDO's phishing-resistant technologies (AAL3) were implemented in most browsers by 2015.

FIDO claims its goal was always to "help reduce the world's over-reliance on passwords" (p. 4). Acknowledging that two-factor authentication apps and roaming devices (i.e. USB keys) aren't likely to be universally adopted by the general population, FIDO is developing an alternative. It claims that "[it] can offer phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication in a single authenticator, thus offering a path toward eliminating passwords for these high-security use cases" (p. 4).

How do they plan to do this? Essentially, FIDO is betting on proximity-based authentication using a Bluetooth-enabled secondary device. Presumably, this secondary device is most likely to be a smartphone, but could also include a tablet, secondary computer, smartwatch, or other hardware. What happens when you replace your secondary device? FIDO addresses this by arguing for the need for easily moving credentials from one device to another, with the help of biometric sensors. "Just like password managers do with passwords, the underlying OS platform will 'sync' the cryptographic keys that belong to a FIDO credential from device to device" (p. 5). In instances where the user has purchased a device from a new vendor (eg. Android to iPhone), Bluetooth is the fallback. FIDO says that "if the... credential isn't readily available on the device from which the user is trying to authenticate, the user will likely have a device (e.g., phone) nearby that does have the credential. The user will then be able to use their existing device to facilitate authentication from their new device" (p. 5).

Lily Hay Newman provides an excellent translation of FIDO's strategy.

Newman points out that moving your 'keys' might not always be a smooth process. A good example would be transferring data from an iPhone to an Android phone. FIDO's proposal does allow for other Bluetooth-enabled devices, like a laptop, to function as your authenticator. But how many secondary devices does the average person have laying around?

Flaws in the approach

An obvious problem with FIDO's plan is that there is likely to be a significant number of people who don't have or don't want a secondary device. For them, a password is much more convenient. And, even if individuals could opt for a Bluetooth-enabled USB key (or some other 'non-smart device') to serve as their authenticator, there's still the possibility that such devices could get lost. How easy is it to retrieve one's login credentials in that event?

FIDO's new security scheme relies heavily on OEMs ('original equipment manufacturers') like Apple, Google, Microsoft, among others to provide security. Brandon Vigliarolo at The Register points out that passwordless systems like this rely on the security of the operating system.

Furthermore, FIDO's plan doesn't suddenly level up our device security to NIST's AAL3 standard. It's just better, and more phishing resistant, than what we have. Do you trust your device or operating system maker to implement FIDO's plan securely? How will we ensure that the FIDO standard is equally secure across platforms? These are questions that need to be answered in the future.

Alternatives for securing our lives

I have strongly recommended two-factor authentication to friends and family, assuming they are not using an SMS solution. (SMS two-factor authentication can be intercepted). While security improvements are laudable, there are other things people can do to secure their digital lives. Until we have a phishing-resistant system, here are some tips.

  1. Do not give any information to people over the phone. Assume that Microsoft, Apple, your bank, or your tax agency will never call you. Hang up on anyone asking for your password to anything.

  2. Reduce the number of online accounts you have. We need to file our taxes, access our bank, send an email, sign in to our Apple ID, and so on. For these 'essential' logins, use two-factor authentication using an authenticator app (if possible) and follow the advice in step For other optional services, particularly shopping sites, consider checking out as a guest or not using the service. Consider having a junk email (not in your name) that you use for these services. If you are an Apple user, use their email hiding feature.

  3. On the advice of security expert Bruce Schneier, write down your passwords down. Password managers like One Password, LastPass, and Bit Warden are great (and I'd use one), but you should have easy access to the passwords for your essential accounts. Memorizing difficult passwords for five essential accounts is doable. Writing them down and hiding them in a secure place is perfectly reasonable. I wouldn't completely rely on an Internet service to protect you.
