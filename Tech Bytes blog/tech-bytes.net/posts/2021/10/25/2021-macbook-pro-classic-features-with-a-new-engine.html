2021 MacBook Pro: Classic features with a new engine

October 25, 2021

By Erik Christiansen

Apple's 'Unleashed' event happened on Oct 18th, and the company finally revealed their updated MacBook Pro laptops running on Apple Silicon. Today, The reviews are starting to trickle out. For a full breakdown of everything Apple announced (including updates to HomePod mini, Apple Music, and AirPods 3), I would encourage you to head over to Jason Snell and Dan Moren's blog Six Colors for the whole scoop. If you want a deep dive video, I'd head over to Rene Richtie's YouTube channel.

In this article, I want to focus on the MacBooks - particularly on the new designs, Apple's mea culpa on ports, the next-gen Apple Silicon, and what this update might foreshadow for the Mac moving forward. Having watched the announcement and read some of these early reviews, I wanted to provide my takeaways. The MacBook Pro is truly a pro machine, and nobody benefits more from their power than creative professionals. But what about enthusiasts or "semi-pro" users? I only dabble in video, audio, and photo editing, and programming is just a hobby for me. Semi-pro is the best I can hope for right now. What MacBook Pro features will benefit Mac enthusiasts and which features are overkill? In this post, I will highlight what I think are the most practical features for these folks, while also discussing the broader implications of this update.

Industrial design and keyboard

MacBook Pro front
MacBook Pro keyboard
MagSafe 3

These new Macs have a very 2010/2011-esque design, and they remind me of the first unibody aluminum laptops Apple launched. Just take a look at the non-Retina Display MacBooks - the ones featuring disc drives. Notice any similarities?

Nilay Patel, at The Verge, compared the new design to the PowerBook G4 titanium - a classic Macintosh by all measures. This is very much a spiritual successor to that design, and it leaves behind the "impossibly thin" but "infinitely impractical" MacBook designs introduced in 2016 (excluding the 2019 model). I would go as far as to say that Apple did some housecleaning in its industrial design department. Bloomberg's Alex Webb argues, quite correctly, that Apple's design has been more practical since the departure of Jony Ive. Good riddance to the obsession with thinness.

The boxy design is a little harsher and more industrial than the rounded look of previous Macs. Although I have yet to handle one of these machines, I find these boxy designs more practical. Aside from the iPad (to which the rounded design was easier to pick up), I find it easier to hold devices with square edges; it seems less slippery, for lack of a better word.

A side note about the black-backed keyboard. I don't understand the controversy around this. It's just a colour. Rene Ritchie made the best critique I've read which is that the black background combined with black keys might pose a contrast problem for individuals with low vision. To see the keys in the dark, we now have to rely entirely on the backlight setup.

The keyboard seems very similar to what you'd find on the 2019 MacBook Pro 16" and 2020 MacBook Pro 13" models. For years, pro Mac users have struggled with or abandoned the Mac due to the poor performance of the butterfly keyboard0. The big difference, aside from the colour, is the removal of the Touch Bar. I never 'disliked' the Touch Bar, but it seemed like an unnecessary feature. Unless you used applications like Better Touch Tool, physical function keys make more sense out of the box.

Display

From what I've read, the mini-LED displays with their increased sustained and peak brightness are a big improvement. I'm not a display aficionado nor am I a professional photo editor. I've always been impressed with the clarity (and durability) of Mac displays and this seems no different. Reference modes will be popular among video and photo editors, as will be the ability to change the display refresh rate. The one upgrade I'm excited to see is the 120Hz ProMotion display. As an iPad Pro user, these high refresh displays are excellent and are considerably easier on the eyes even when working with something as banal as a word processor. Regardless of your use case, this might be a very useful upgrade if you spend many hours on a computer. I'm confident that by investing in high-resolution displays for almost ten years I've delayed the need for glasses.

Unfortunately, these displays have a notch. My opinion is that this is bad. I use a lot of menu bar apps, and the notch would certainly intrude once I have them all working.

Apple tried to spin the notch as additional space, but I predict it will be more of a nuisance - especially given that the camera sensor doesn't feature Face ID (for unlocking the laptop), centre stage, or a 4k webcam. However, the 1080p webcam Apple included seems 'fine', and I still prefer Touch ID authentication. I would have preferred a larger top bezel like my 2019 MacBook Pro 16". Thinner bezels here seem like a solution in search of a problem.

Ports

HDMI port, SD card reader, and MagSafe 3 charging
MagSafe 3 charging

Aside from the new design, Apple has capitulated in terms of ports. There are three USB-C / Thunderbolt 4 ports. These can be used for charging as well as data. More exciting, Apple has resurrected MagSafe (now called MagSafe 3) - the proprietary but practical magnetic charging connector. This time, MagSafe is attached to the power brick via USB-C meaning the system is a bit more modular. If you damage the MagSafe cable, you don't have to purchase a whole new charging brick, just the cable. These MagSafe connectors also do fast charging, though the charging specs are different between the 14" and 16" models. The lesson here is this: Proprietary charging is fine if it adds value. I would love to see MagSafe 3 implemented on the iPhone or iPad in addition to USB-C. A single charger for everything would be amazing.

Apple also included HDMI and an SD card slot. I'm currently working on a 2019 MacBook Pro 16" which has been great. I don't use a lot of ports except for an HDMI adapter to connect to my 4k display. I rarely connect my MacBook to displays when I'm travelling, so my USB-C to HDMI dongle is essentially a permanent fixture in my home office. That being said, I appreciate the frustration of carrying a bag of dongles everywhere you go to connect to projectors or pull data off an SD card. Regardless if you're a pro or an enthusiast, more ports are a win. Even if you just need to connect your external monitor, chances are that it's HDMI.

These are all welcome changes, but I can't say I'm impressed - just pleased. The photographers and video editors of the world need ports like HDMI and SD card slots. My 2013 MacBook Pro 13" - which was handed down to my wife and is still going strong - already had these "legacy" ports. Apple went too far when it tried to force an all USB-C future with its 2015 MacBook release. I'm glad to see this refresh, but it's simply a return to baseline. It's not worthy of too much praise because the pro community has been complaining about the lack of ports for six years. I'm content. Apple, keep up the good work.

Apple Silicon updates

The star of the show isn't the design or ports, but Apple Silicon. The new M1 Pro and M1 Max look to be excellent performers, but I do look forward to thorough comparisons from AnandTech and ArsTechnica. Essentially, we're getting Mac Pro desktop-level performance inside a laptop. In every respect, from speed to power consumption these chips are superior to their Intel variants. Just look at this table regarding video export speed and battery usage from Rene.

I'm not as qualified to speak to the technical aspects of the new M1 Pro and Max chips. For a full breakdown, I would suggest Rene's overview. Technical specs aside, the implications for the future of the Mac are most interesting.

Regardless of your use case, an investment in Apple Silicon MacBook Pros (or MacBook Air) will assure that you get years of software support. It's unknown how long Apple will support Intel machines. Though there's a lot of Intel Macs in the world and they're still being sold on Apple's website. If keep your machines for a long time, the M1 Pro and Max will probably get you a solid 5-7 years out of these machines.

The M1 Pro and M1 Max foreshadow what we can expect from Apple's upcoming pro desktop chips. I expect that the larger iMac (27" to 31"), the Mac Pro, and pro-Mac Mini will all feature variations of these chips. It's important to remember that the MacBook Pros, despite their power, are constrained by battery life. An Apple Silicon Mac that's plugged into AC power and has a chassis more conducive to cooling is likely to be impressive. If these MacBook Pro chips offer a huge bump in performance over current desktop Intel chips, it's mind-boggling to imagine what Apple's upcoming desktops will be capable of.

Missing features and other observations

These practical updates should set aside any fears that "Apple is doomed" without Jony Ive at the helm of the design department. I will take beautiful and practical over 'thinner' every day.

One more comment on the ports. They're a great addition, but given the increased thickness, I wonder if it would be beneficial to include one or two of them on the back rather than the side. A MagSafe connector and HDMI on the back would make cable management easier.

Also, I noticed that the "MacBook Pro" label has once again disappeared from the design. The 2011 model had it, the 2012 model removed it, the 2016 model put it back, and now we've removed it again. Why is this? Is there a design battle going on about branding that I'm not aware of?

Conclusion

In summary, this is a superb update. Pros got what they were asking for and the performance is better than anyone expected. I see no reason not to be excited if you're a Mac user.

Whether you should upgrade or not is more complicated. MacBook Pros are for professionals. What about the rest of us wannabes? If you have a relatively recent Intel MacBook that doesn't cause you grief and has a working battery, I'd say wait for another generation (or two). There are a lot of Intel Macs in the world, and while I can attest that Apple Silicon provides better responsiveness, your text editor or office suites won't run that much better. Intel Macs weren't rendered useless following this update. However, if you are someone who edits video - even at the enthusiast level - these new laptops might be worth it.

These machines are expensive, and given the performance of M1 (the entry-level chip), this upgrade makes me wonder if enthusiasts need a pro machine at all. For many years Apple's "pro" machines were pro in name only. From 2016 to 2018 enthusiasts had to purchase pro machines to get anything more than adequate performance. This is in stark contrast to the 2010-2017 MacBook Air which could be outfitted with "almost pro" Intel chips and RAM. Today, we've returned to an era where enthusiasts and semi-pros might be served better by waiting for the next MacBook Air/MacBook Pro 13" upgrades to M2. Outfitted with enough RAM and storage, these consumer devices could likely handle anything an enthusiast could throw at them for a fraction of the price.
