The value of offline functionality

July 20, 2022

Recently, Canadians across Canada experienced a massive Internet network outage due to a disruption in Rogers Communications services. Ultimately, the outage should have reinforced two facts.

  1. We are incredibly dependent on the Internet.

The mainstream media is mostly focused on point 1, and for good reason. The Rogers outage disrupted millions of dollars worth of commerce and made it difficult for many folks to do their jobs.

The disruption affected approximately ten million Canadians, and cost as much as $142 million in commerce, and crippled much of the infrastructure including mobile phones, home Internet, business payment systems, concerts in Toronto, and ATMs.

  1. The importance of preserving offline functionality

What I took away from this outage (I wasn't affected thankfully) is that offline functionality in my devices is critical.

I'm from a generation (maybe even the last generation), that remembers when personal computers were primarily offline devices. It's not my intent to scold younger folks, nor am I suggesting that I was born during a better time. It's just a fact that most home computers from the 1990s - which were generally shared by all family members - were offline most of the time. As a result, our software was designed with offline use in mind. Budgeting, word processing, and gaming were all done offline.

Dial-up Internet was an amazing advancement, but it was inconvenient. Where I grew up on Vancouver Island, British Columbia, my family subscribed to a local Internet provider whose name escapes me. If memory services me correctly, we got a maximum number of minutes we could use each month - similar to pay-as-you-go cell phone plans. Using the Internet between midnight and 6 am didn't count against your minutes, so a lot of homework and personal research was done during non-peak times. Using the Internet during the day was somewhat discouraged because dial-up connections disrupted your landline phone. Having a second phone life for Internet access was a luxury back then. Eventually, our family moved onto broadband in the early 2000s and this was no longer a problem.

The consequence of dial-up Internet was that our computer was primarily an offline device. For example, if I was writing a paper for school, I was focused almost entirely on my Microsoft Word document. My research would have mostly come from my school library (physical books), and any Internet sources I used would have been printed or read beforehand. When connecting to the Internet, I had to tell the family (or ask for permission), as it would disrupt incoming calls. It was also critical that I logged off when I was finished online, otherwise I'd inadvertently use up the family's Internet minutes.

The irony today is that there's a host of applications that block distracting websites, since constant and unlimited Internet access has made us less productive. When I was a kid, the Internet was treated like a grocery store. You were in and out. You found the information you needed and then logged off. The early days of smartphones were similar to the dial-up Internet era because speeds were slow and data plans were limited.

Contrast what I'm describing to the current era where everyone has a computer in their pocket and an Internet connection is often required. Traditional computers (laptops and desktops) still have the offline utility I've described above. It's in their DNA. But have you ever noticed how useless your phone or tablet is when you don't have a connection? The Rogers outage highlighted this fact for several colleagues who commented that their mobile devices were reduced to thousand-dollar paperweights. To be fair, mobile devices are much more competent offline than they used to be, but there are still annoyances that I run into regularly. Even desktop computers suffer from 'required online connections'. Consider the following examples where the Internet is required, but shouldn't be.

  • Verifying a game license or constant connection is required even to play a single player.
  • Verify a productivity application license or require an Internet connection to use for offline purposes.
  • Applications that require constant syncing to a cloud service instead of using local storage.
  • Apps that are a 'wrapper' for web content and don't hold offline data. Eg. Travel apps and news apps.

What should be done

Several changes would make our devices more useful without an Internet connection. The first is to make our software less dependent on Internet verification or require a constant connection. I can understand registering digital software right after purchase. For example, let's say you downloaded the free version of a piece of software and you want to upgrade to the pro version. Typically, you can upgrade the license somewhere in the settings of the program. In this situation, it makes sense to have an Internet connection because you have to authorize the payment via credit card and activate the license. You'd need an Internet connection to download updates to that software. But outside of those situations, the software should not require a constant online connection to function. The same logic can be applied to single-player games (both desktop and console games). Why do I need to be logged into some game developer's server to play a game alone in my basement?

The second change is how mobile operating systems and apps are designed. The desktop computer is the model for offline functionality, but our iPhone, iPads, and Android devices are lagging. There has been significant work done by Apple on their Files app which is a decent file management system. The more mobile file systems incorporate desktop features the better. The ability to create files, name them, and save them in a folder structure of your design without ever being asked to connect to the Internet is ideal. At this point, most mobile apps for content creation allow for this flexibility, and they only require Internet syncing when you want to back up to the cloud. Apps that are more consumption oriented, such as newspaper apps, understandably require an Internet connection to download the latest content. The option to have new content periodically downloaded for offline reading (similar to ebooks, audiobooks, and podcasts) would go a long way to make us less Internet dependent.

The Internet is amazing. I wouldn't be able to write and share this piece with anyone but myself without the Internet. But despite all the advantages of online communication, there's still value in having offline capability - for security and mental health reasons. Ironically, I think we'd all be a bit more productive, and maybe breath easier, if we logged off of the Internet when we don't need it.
