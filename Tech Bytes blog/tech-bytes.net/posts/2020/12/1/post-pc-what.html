Post-PC what?

December 1, 2020

By Erik Christiansen﻿

Now here’s a term that I haven’t heard in a while. “Post-PC.” Horis Dediu discusses the term in his recent blog post at Asymco.

For those not familiar, the “Post-PC era” was supposed to be a societal shift away from traditional desktop computing toward a more mobile world. As Dediu points out, the term Post-PC was popularized by David D. Clark from MIT. Steve Jobs also popularized the idea, as it was in the context of the Post-PC era that he discussed the iPod, iPhone, and iPad (more so the latter two). 

Jobs famously argued at the D10 conference that the personal computer (including Mac and Windows) was like a truck. A workhorse for any given situation. And like trucks, PCs wouldn’t be the favoured vehicle type forever. Jobs theorized that other devices, like the smartphone and tablet, would steal away the more casual users. PCs would still be around, but they would remain the work devices (the trucks) while phones and tablets would be the cars.

Dediu points out that mobile devices have experienced a ten-fold increase in sales while PCs have stagnated over the past ten years. Though PCs haven’t gone away, there isn’t as much growth. He poses an interesting question. What about the Mac? Since Apple popularized Post-PC devices with the iPhone and iPad, wouldn’t the Mac be the first to disappear? Not so much.

As the graph above demonstrates, the Mac has outpaced the iPad - the poster boy of the Post-PC devices. Macs (and laptops generally) are popular trucks indeed.

Where I might disagree with Dediu, is his assessment of how PCs define themselves. He’s correct that we measure PCs by traditional dimensions of performance which are different from Post-PC devices. Dediu is also correct in his assessment that cellular connectivity is strangely absent from laptops. But some of these dimensions such as heart rate monitors, cameras, step and altitude counters don’t seem particularly relevant to the PC. Are these fair comparisons? This is a small difference of opinion. Two metrics PCs and Post-PC devices are measures on are power and efficiency. These come down to the silicon present in each machine, and the last ten years have shown the world that phones and tablets have seen incredible advancements where PCs haven’t.

I think Dediu is correct in his assessment that Apple Silicon might result in new form factors and could challenge our conception of the PC. I think he points out something that’s often forgotten, that the PC is the preferred device for authoring. Like him, I’m not composing this article using my iPad. I could, but it would be more challenging. Flipping between Asymco and Squarespace is easier on a Mac or Windows laptop. But, as Dediu says, the tablet (namely the iPad) has taken a portion of my computing time away from my ‘truck’ laptop - namely email, casual games, messaging, watching videos, etc. As the tablet gets better, in theory, it would take away more of the ‘low end’ computing tasks, leaving the PC to pursue more high-end tasks.

Apple Silicon turns this notion on its head. PC stagnation was partially due to a lack of innovation. But, if a new chip architecture (on either the Mac or Windows side) allows for more PC innovation, one could make the argument that we might see a surge in innovation. What would that do to Post-PC devices?

Reflecting on the state of the market reminds me of how silly the term ‘Post-PC’ really is. Everything is a PC. Some just have more screen real estate and keyboards while others are touch-based. These touch devices are designed for consumption rather than production. Microsoft, and its PC partners, have been bucking the trend with 2-in-1 devices for seven years. Is the Surface a tablet or a PC? It depends on who you talk to.

As I reflect on ‘Post-PC’ and Dediu’s article, I wonder if that term is best used to describe the current technology holding pattern. Today, we live in a multi-device world. But, this world is shrinking. You can see this with the introduction of the smartphone. That single device replaced my address book, mp3 player, camera, and (let’s be honest) Nintendo Game Boy. All these devices were replaced by a tiny hand-held PC. Without sounding too post-modern, is a ‘Post Post-PC’ era a return to even fewer devices? That sounds a lot like the PC era. Do the Post-PC devices become irrelevant because PCs, through more efficient ARM processors and better connectivity, cease being trucks but transformers? It’s an interesting thought experiment.

However you view the future of the PC, there certainly seems to be a renewed interest in this antiquated form factor. It’s kind of like the book. Perhaps there’s something so essential about the PC that it can’t be replaced.
