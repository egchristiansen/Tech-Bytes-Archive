Apple's M1 chip: Making desktop computing cool again

November 29, 2020

By Erik Christiansen

My intention was not for this website to become the Apple blog, but the famous fruit company seems to be dominating much of the news cycle this year.

This past summer, at the World Wide Developer Conference (WWDC), Apple announced that it would be transitioning from Intel to its in-house Apple Silicon over the next two years. Apple said the first Macs with in-house silicon would arrive by the end of 2020. Well, here we are, and we have a new MacBook Air, 13 inch MacBook Pro, and a Mac mini.

The form factors of these devices are identical, but the real magic is the new M1 chip inside. Is it as fast as Apple claims? Will it run our apps properly? What does the M1 chip mean for desktop computing generally? In this article, I will try and answer these questions.

The importance of Apple Silicon

As I outlined in my articles about Apple Silicon and the broader industry move from ARM to x86, Apple's transition from Intel to in-house silicon is significant. Apple hasn't made a platform shift in fifteen years, not since it switched from IBM's PowerPC architecture to Intel in 2005/2006.

This change is also significant because it puts traditional desktop computing centre stage for the first time in a decade. Devindra Hardawar from Engadget sums it up perfectly.

What is the M1 chip?

"M1" is the name of Apple's new in-house Mac chip. This ARM-based CPU is being used in Apple's 'entry-level' Macs, including the MacBook Air, Mac Mini, and MacBook Pro. Once again, Apple is blurring the line between consumer and 'pro' devices.

All chips have tradeoffs. Typically, it comes down to performance vs efficiency. Low power Intel chips might provide better battery life, but performance takes a hit. The previous Intel MacBook Air was a perfect example. M1 delivers a considerable performance advantage and outstanding battery life. As noted by Are Technica, M1 stays cooler than Intel, even when under load.

Apple opted for a fanless design in the MacBook Air. As my podcast co-host Kris Hans notes, the MacBook Air is essentially an "iPad in a clamshell design" running macOS. The fan in the MacBook Pro and Mac Mini allows the M1 chip to run faster and longer, before having to throttle down due to heat. However, it's worth noting that the difference is minimal. The MacBook Air still delivers incredible performance, and a casual user probably wouldn't even notice.

So many benchmarks

A constant among the reviews so far is that the M1 chip outclasses almost all Intel x86 chips on the market - with a few exceptions.

According to Apple Insider, Apple "has never put a strong emphasis on the gigahertz race." The M1 has one configuration in all three machines, meaning the user can only change the RAM and SSD configurations when purchasing a new computer. I'm confident future pro machines will allow for more configurations, but for consumer machines, this is a return to form for Apple. The simpler options remind me of Steve Jobs' four-by-four matrix which was intended to simplify Apple's confusing product line. Consumers don't care about gigahertz so why not make it simple for them?

In terms of raw performance, the M1 chip is incredible. According to Apple Insider, it outperforms pretty much every Intel competitor.

For the full M1 benchmarks, Six Colors and Ars Technica have the most detailed breakdowns.

Battery life is also improved thanks to M1. At this point, I've read reviews by veteran Mac users such as John Gruber, as well as several professional video editors and photographers. Jason Snell from Six Colors reports nine and a half hours of battery life for the new Air and a bit more for the new 13-inch Pro. Colin Smith from Photoshop Cafe reported little battery drain even during the initial setup of the computer which involved downloading and installing gigabytes of apps. Office workers can expect to get a full workday out of the battery.

Perhaps what most incredible about the M1 chip is its ability to remain cool under load. As a happy MacBook Pro 16-inch owner, I can report that this laptop generally runs cool but certainly heads up when exporting audio or video, or when working in photoshop. John Gruber notes that M1 never really heats up during regular use, and barely so during multi-core benchmarking.

How the M1 uses memory has also changed. With the Unified Memory Architecture, M1 (like Intel chips) has a CPU and integrated graphics. The computer memory is "shared by both processor cores and graphics cores," according to Jason Snell. But, this is where the similarity ends.

This means that the Mac configuration you choose is final. Mac laptops have had soldered memory for a long time, making upgrades difficult (or impossible) except for the most proficient computer technicians. Now, the prospect of upgrading memory is gone because everything lives on a single chip.

In terms of day-to-day performance, there is nothing to indicate that the average user would even know these Macs run on a new architecture. Rosetta 2, Apple's translation software, allows Apple Silicon Macs to run x86 apps. All indications point to roughly equivalent, or slightly slower, performance while running x86 apps on the M1. Six Colors reported x86 apps running at about 80 percent as fast as native code. Keep in mind this 80 percent was still faster than many Intel Macs. Apps designed for Apple Silicon run considerably faster, so things will only get better with time. Here is what John Gruber said about Rosetta 2.

One advantage Apple Silicon Macs will have over their Intel brethren is the ability to run iPhone and iPad apps on the desktop. It's up to the developer if these apps appear on the Mac App Store, but the prospect is interesting.

The big question going into this transition was "can Apple's ARM-based chips scale for a laptop or desktop computer?" Most definitely yes. It appears that Apple Silicon has no problem scaling up to compete with desktop-class x86 chips. Also, keep in mind that the M1 is the entry-level chip. In his YouTube video, Colin Smith said current Intel Mac owners shouldn't run out and purchase an M1 device for this reason. Imagine what an "M2" chip would be like? What if the iMac ships with two M1 chips? There's every reason to suspect that the performance gap between Apple Silicon and Intel will widen with the introduction of professional-grade chips.

Design

Leading up to the release of Apple Silicon Macs, tech pundits were speculating that Apple would introduce a radically redesigned computer chassis for all upcoming laptops and desktops. I was suspicious, and my suspicions were correct. These devices are identical to their Intel predecessors, and they even include the same terrible 720p webcams.

Why didn't Apple redesign these devices? I suspect it comes down to cost and the fact that these are first-gen products. When a company redesigns a device, that requires major changes to the manufacturing process and increases cost. Considering the current Mac laptop designs aren't that old (updated as recently as 2019 and 2020), there's no reason to throw them out. Also, first-generation Apple products are essentially public betas. Regardless of the performance or 'cool factor', my advice to most people is to avoid first-gen Apple products. These devices are often imperfect. Some notable examples not great first-gen Apple products include the iPad, iPhone, iPhone, 2016 MacBook Pro, etc. Even if a first-gen device is reliable, Apple tends to be conservative in its initial designs. It won't be until the second-generation Apple Silicon Macs that we start to see new form factors. Given how fast and cool these chips run, you can expect to see thinner and lighter devices both on the consumer and professional side. This is a practice round, but so far Apple is hitting it out of the park.

Industry response

This is an exciting time to be a computing enthusiast. The Mac, and the desktop computing industry overall, has largely been ignored for ten years. Phones and tablets have dominated the conversation. Yet, these "post-PC" devices never replaced our desktop appliances, and I think Apple knows that. My phone is great. My iPad is great. They're both essential tools that I use daily. But there is something unsatisfying about them. As incredible as they are, they don't have the geek appeal of desktop or laptop computers. Laptops have screens, keyboards, ports, and different configurations. From an enthusiast's perspective, they give me more to talk about.

Thankfully, it looks like the M1 chip has reinvigorated desktop computing after many years of stagnation and incremental improvements. This is probably the biggest leap in computing performance I've witnessed in my lifetime.

Yet, I can't help but consider the downside of Apple Silicon, mainly that it's locked behind Apple's walled garden. Even before these M1 laptops, Apple had greatly improved the Mac laptop line which has suffered from glitchy keyboards, poor thermal designs, and high prices (relative to the competition) since 2016. Tough competition from excellent Windows devices, not to mention that professionals were switching to Windows, pushed Apple to be better. Apple doesn't seem to innovate unless there is strong competition, and the same could be said about most companies. With M1 lapping the competition, where is the motivation to compete? How could Intel, AMD, or Qualcomm catch up to Apple Silicon? I don't think they can.

This means that Windows laptops and desktop will be considerably slower than the Mac until there is a comparable computing breakthrough. I'm sure there are engineers inside Microsoft who have the skill to develop compelling custom chips for their Surface devices and Windows. But, Microsoft is in the unenviable position of having to make Windows run on a broad spectrum of hardware including Intel. I don't see how they could support a version of Windows just for Surface on ARM and the rest of the industry. Microsoft's flagship Windows ARM device, the Surface Pro X, has lacklustre performance and doesn't run x86 applications nearly as well as Apple Silicon.

Another possibility is that Apple Silicon will light a fire under both Microsoft and Qualcomm to make Windows on ARM competitive. Veteran Microsoft journalist Paul Thurrott explained on the Windows Weekly podcast in July that Windows on ARM is actually "Windows on Snapdragon." The ARM implementation Microsoft has pursued is so custom to Qualcomm's chipset that the project name "Windows on ARM" is misleading. Until this point, there has been no motivator for Microsoft to develop ARM compatibility further. A more competitive Qualcomm and Windows laptop space will keep the industry exciting. Without that competition, Apple will - once again - rest on its laurels.

The broader implication of Apple Silicon will probably mean the eventual death of the x86 architecture if Windows devices move to ARM. For productivity applications, there is every indication that ARM can perform just as well as x86. ARM appears to be able to scale up, while x86 seems incapable of scaling down for low power devices. The biggest holdout is gaming. Game development is not my area of expertise. Engines like Unity have been optimized to run on ARM, and the Nintendo Switch has demonstrated that a relatively low-power device can run AAA titles - albeit with some visual downgrades. It's hard to see how x86 isn't the antiquated platform at this point.

While ARM is certainly promising and is the more exciting platform, it's hard for me to believe that x86 doesn't have clear advantages. Desktop towers, and laptops, have benefited greatly from AMD's Ryzen CPUs. For many years, AMD was the underdog in its battle with Intel. It seems a shame to abandon the x86 platform shortly after AMD managed to innovate using that platform. Having programs that work across platforms seems like a better strategy than shifting an entire industry to a new architecture.

Something is unsettling about platform transitions. It's messy. Those of us who've lived through such transitions before cannot help but have nightmares of incompatibility. Changing the platform standards by which all computing is based is a big deal. How do we transition our stuff? It's not unlike moving to a new house.

But, there is every indication to be hopeful about this transition. It's an exciting time to be alive if you care about technology. Desktop computing is finally cool again.
