OS cross-pollination and what it means for the Mac and iPad

July 17, 2020

*Part of the WWDC 2020 series*

By Erik Christiansen

If you've been a Mac user for a long time, you know that the community can be fickle. On one hand, there's a consistent complaint that the Mac platform doesn't get nearly enough attention from Apple compared to iOS/iPadOS. On the other hand, this community is resistant to change. Every time there any significant changes to macOS’ look and feel, no matter how small, there's seems to be immediate skepticism. The design changes coming to the Mac signify something bigger. It’s being introduced as part of a broader vision that will be brought forth when all Macs transition to Apple Silicon.

But change can be good. The changes coming to macOS Big Sur are divisive because the OS is clearly adopting a more iPad-like look and feel. It’s going to be different, and I would argue it’s a much more drastic design overhaul than the introduction of Yosemite in 2014. Many folks have focused on the iPad influences on the Mac, but I'd argue there's more of a cross-pollination between these platforms. It’s clear to me that the platforms aren’t merging (at least not yet). But both are borrowing features from each other. This is an ecosystem play. These design changes will make it considerably easier to switch between the Mac and iPad, making owners of both happy campers. I'm not brave enough to install beta software on my primary machines, but from what I can tell macOS Big Sur and iPadOS 14 tells us a lot about the future of these platforms.

The changes…

This change to macOS Big Sur is either a curse or a blessing depending on which side of the aisle you're on. Long-time macOS enthusiasts might dislike the fact that the Mac no longer has the "unique look and feel" compared to the iPhone and iPad. Others might prefer this new design because the Mac was starting to feel antiquated. 

I think for new users, the merging of these design languages is a plus because it makes switching between devices easier. There are clear visual changes throughout macOS, but several examples stand out. If you want a more complete visual comparison, I'd recommend Andrew Denty's macOS Catalina vs Big Sur comparison.

The Dock icons on macOS Catalina are strange. Since macOS Yosemite, the icons had the classic look of macOS’ past, but with an almost unnecessary level of detail. In Big Sur, the icons are more aligned with what you’d expect on the iPhone or iPad. A perfect example is messages. It used to be blue on the Mac and green everywhere else. Mail, the App Store, Reminders, Notes, etc. now are instantly recognizable. Whatever you think about this change, it’s hard to argue that consistency is a problem.

I think this is a fair compromise. At a glance, the new icons are easier to recognize between the devices, but there's still a unique feel to the macOS versions.

Perhaps what’s more interesting about the icon design is what it says about Apple’s general design language moving forward. An article in Input Magazine (a design and UX publication) described Apple's new design language as a move to "neumorphism". Early versions of iOS (iPhone and iPad) and OS X had a skeuomorphic design. To indicate to the user what apps and settings did, the apps, icons and settings were represented by relatable real-world objects. For instance, the original YouTube app (developed by Apple) looked like a vintage TV set. The podcast app was a tape reel. The calendar app had perforated and torn pages and a stitched leader backdrop (like a business portfolio). 

With iOS 7 in 2013, Apple completely switched tracks and went for a very non-skeuomorphic and 'flat' design. Real-world analogies were removed and replaced by... well pretty meaningless icons. I actually think the iOS 7 ‘flatness’ was a step backward for usability because all sense of depth, colour and obvious signposting throughout the OS were eliminated. It was simply harder to navigate. Today, iOS and iPad OS have that flat feel, but many of the accessibility and usability aspects have been added back - making it a very refined and pleasant experience overall. OSX Daily wrote a great visual comparison of this switch from skeuomorphic design to the ‘flat’ design.

Some designers argue that now Apple is moving toward this neumorphism design on the Mac, and perhaps this is foreshadowing what’s to come on its other platforms. Neumorphism stays with the trend of not having real-world analogies that teach the user how to navigate the OS, but tries to make the OS more tactile and interactive. Light and shadow are used to highlight depth which is important for telling users where buttons, overlapping menus/windows and icons are.

To me, neumorphism looks very 'marshmallow'. It has that look of embossed paper. The new dock icons Apple has introduced don't go this far, but the influence is clearly there. All the new default dock icons look like those on an iPad, except with more depth and shadow.

The Mac menu bar is now very transparent. I'm not sure this change is an improvement. The menu bar in macOS Catalina has enough transparency in my opinion. It’s subtle, but the backgrounds one chooses for the desktop do affect the coloration of the menu bar. But, the completely transparent menu bar is more in alignment with - and might be a half step towards - the iPhone/iPad menu bar which has no visible dividing line. 

The most interesting aspect of the new menu bar is the ability to add widgets from the notification/widgets panel. Though I wonder if this creates duplicated information. I always found the Mac Dashboard (a feature which was finally removed) to be a bit redundant given that the menu bar also allowed access to “glanceable” information. The widgets themselves very closely resemble those on the iPad, and they also come in different sizes. The Control Centre in Big Sur is also almost entirely lifted from the iPad. I can't help but notice the larger touch targets here which make me think that a touchscreen Mac might be possible in the future.

The Mac Finder toolbar controls also resemble those in the iOS/iPad OS Files app - again, making for more consistency when switching between devices. System Preferences, which doesn’t have an iPad equivalent, still looks more iPad-like in my opinion. Regardless of what one might think of the new design, it’s clearly been consistently implemented across the system.

Even Safari on Big Sur has taken a page from the iPad (again look at that toolbar) with the inclusion of a more customizable start page and general look and feel. Once again, privacy is also a focal point of Apple’s browser, as it now actively provides a breakdown of what websites are tracking you and how.

The examples of macOS borrowing from iPadOS could span several blog posts, but what I’ve mentioned are the most drastic changes. All of the original Catalyst apps (those mac Apps ported from the iPad a few years ago) will get a similar overhaul in terms of look and feel.

Of course, the cross-pollination isn't a one-way street. The iPad is borrowing considerably from the Mac. Sidebars have been integrated into the Notes and Photos app, and the look and feel are much more "Mac-like" overall. Furthermore, the updated spotlight search is almost identical to the Mac version, as it now shows up as a floating window rather than taking over the whole screen. Perhaps floating windows are a possibility for the iPad in the future?

What this means for the future of the platforms

In a recent Macworld article, long-time Apple enthusiast Jason Snell said the following.

I think his observation is correct. He and other technology pundits have been speculating that Apple and Microsoft are chasing the same goal, but they are coming at it from different directions. Microsoft opted for turning Windows (both 8 and 10) into the one-size-fits-all OS for both keyboard/mouse and touchscreen devices. While the Windows experience is not ideal for touch, they only have to make one OS. This makes sense given the huge range of devices Microsoft needs to support. On the other side, Apple has approached this problem by developing and maintaining two platforms in tandem - macOS and iOS/iPadOS. (I’m putting aside the Apple Watch and TV for the time being). For a long time, I felt like Apple has a losing strategy. I really felt that not having included touch on the Mac was a mistake, and perhaps they bet on the wrong horse. That is until they switched from Intel to Apple Silicon.

In my previous article, I talked about why Apple Silicon was so significant and pointed out the many subtle business decisions Apple made since 2013 that led them to this point. These include the move to 64-bit apps (mobile and desktop), Catalyst for developing cross-platform apps, Metal for graphics, etc. The big boulder in the way was the dependence on Intel. Moving forward, cross-pollination between the platforms will be easier because everything will run on the same architecture. Windows, unfortunately, will probably remain a less than ideal OS that works everywhere, while Apple will have cross-pollinated its platforms - allowing them to borrow the best features that work in each context but also keeping the platforms distinct. While macOS Big Sur clearly takes a big page from iPadOS, both in terms of design language and features, it still looks like a Mac to my eyes. Similarly, the features ported to the iPad don’t detract from the fact that it's a touchscreen tablet first, and a laptop replacement second. In fact, what Apple really ended up with was two platforms that will have many shared features and run on the same silicon architecture; yet each is different enough that it makes sense on their respective form factor.

While the design changes may be divisive, I’d argue that macOS was starting to look dated, as well an inconsistent with Apple's other products. This isn't to say previous versions of macOS were not functional, but features shared between the Mac and iOS/iPadOS didn't look or function the same. As someone who spends a great deal of time coaching students on these features, it was clear the design differences were a barrier.

The changes to macOS Big Sur and iPadOS effectively unify all the platforms from an interface guidelines perspective. WWDC already revealed that iPad and iPhone apps could run naively on upcoming Macs with Apple Silicon. But, by unifying the design language, continuing to develop its developer tools (notably UI Kit) and using the same silicon I think it’s going to become much easier for Apple to continue to share features across the platforms and for developers to create apps that work everywhere.
